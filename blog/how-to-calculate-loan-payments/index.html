How to Calculate Loan Payments Step by Step

Updated March 2026

Before you sign up for any loan, you should know exactly how much your monthly payment will be. Whether you are financing a car, taking out a student loan, or applying for a mortgage, calculating loan payments in advance helps you budget properly and compare offers from different lenders. This guide breaks down the math behind monthly loan payments in simple terms, with worked examples you can follow along with. By the end, you will be able to evaluate any loan offer with confidence.

What Are Loan Payments?

A loan payment is the fixed amount you pay each month to repay borrowed money. Each payment covers two things: a portion of the original amount borrowed (the principal) and the interest charged by the lender. In the early months, a larger share of your payment goes toward interest. As the loan matures, more of each payment goes toward reducing the principal.

This type of repayment is called an amortized loan, and it is the standard structure for personal loans, car loans, student loans, and mortgages. The monthly amount stays the same from the first payment to the last.

Tip: Even though your monthly payment stays the same, the split between principal and interest changes each month. In the first payment of a 30-year mortgage, over 70% might go to interest. By the final years, over 90% goes to principal. Understanding this helps you see why early extra payments have such a big impact.

Why Calculating Loan Payments Matters

  • Budget accurately: Knowing your exact monthly obligation prevents financial surprises and helps you plan your spending.
  • Compare loan offers: Different lenders offer different rates and terms. Calculating payments for each option shows you which deal actually costs less over time.
  • Understand total cost: A low monthly payment over a long term can cost far more in total interest than a higher payment over a shorter term.
  • Avoid over-borrowing: Running the numbers first helps you determine how much you can comfortably afford before committing.
  • Plan for early payoff: Knowing the math helps you see how extra payments can reduce your interest costs and shorten the loan term.

The Loan Payment Formula

The standard formula for calculating a fixed monthly loan payment is:

M = P [ r(1+r)^n ] / [ (1+r)^n - 1 ]

Where:

  • M = monthly payment
  • P = principal (the amount you borrow)
  • r = monthly interest rate (annual rate divided by 12)
  • n = total number of monthly payments (loan term in years x 12)

How to Calculate Loan Payments: Step-by-Step

Let us work through a concrete example. You borrow $20,000 at 7% annual interest for 5 years.

Example: Car Loan Payment

  1. Identify your values. P = $20,000, annual rate = 7%, term = 5 years.
  2. Calculate the monthly interest rate. r = 7% / 12 = 0.07 / 12 = 0.005833
  3. Calculate total number of payments. n = 5 years x 12 months = 60 payments.
  4. Plug into the formula. M = 20,000 x [0.005833 x (1.005833)^60] / [(1.005833)^60 - 1]
  5. Calculate (1+r)^n. (1.005833)^60 = 1.41762
  6. Calculate the numerator. 0.005833 x 1.41762 = 0.008269. Then 20,000 x 0.008269 = 165.38
  7. Calculate the denominator. 1.41762 - 1 = 0.41762
  8. Divide to get the monthly payment. 165.38 / 0.41762 = $396.02 per month.

Over 60 months, you pay $396.02 x 60 = $23,761.20 total. That means $3,761.20 goes to interest on top of the $20,000 principal.

Example: Comparing Loan Terms

Borrow $15,000 at 6% interest. See how the term length affects your total cost:

  • 3-year term (36 payments): Monthly payment = $456.33, Total paid = $16,427.88, Total interest = $1,427.88
  • 5-year term (60 payments): Monthly payment = $289.99, Total paid = $17,399.40, Total interest = $2,399.40
  • 7-year term (84 payments): Monthly payment = $219.35, Total paid = $18,425.40, Total interest = $3,425.40

The 7-year term has a monthly payment that is $237 less than the 3-year term, but you pay nearly $2,000 more in total interest. Always weigh monthly affordability against total cost.

Tips and Best Practices

  • Compare total cost, not just monthly payments. A longer loan term means lower monthly payments but much more total interest. Always look at the overall cost before deciding.
  • Consider making extra payments. Even small additional amounts each month can dramatically reduce your total interest and shorten the payoff period. An extra $50/month on a $20,000 loan at 7% can save over $600 in interest and pay off the loan 7 months early.
  • Check for prepayment penalties. Some loans charge fees for paying off early. Confirm this before committing to extra payments.
  • Factor in all costs. Beyond the monthly payment, account for loan origination fees, insurance requirements, and other charges that add to the real cost of borrowing.
  • Keep your debt-to-income ratio healthy. Financial advisors generally recommend that total monthly debt payments stay below 36% of your gross monthly income. Exceeding this ratio can make it harder to qualify for future loans.
  • Shop around for rates. Even a 0.5% difference in interest rate can save hundreds or thousands over the life of a loan. Use the calculator to compare multiple offers side by side before making a decision.
  • Consider a larger down payment. For car loans and mortgages, a bigger down payment reduces the principal you need to finance, which lowers both your monthly payment and total interest paid.

Tip: Before applying for a loan, check your credit score. Borrowers with higher scores (740+) typically qualify for significantly lower interest rates. Improving your credit score by even 50 points before applying could save you thousands over the life of the loan.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Only looking at monthly payments. A $200/month payment sounds cheaper than $350/month, but if the first loan runs 10 years and the second runs 3 years, the cheaper payment costs far more overall.
  • Forgetting to convert rates. The formula uses the monthly rate, not the annual rate. Always divide the annual percentage by 12.
  • Ignoring the impact of term length. Stretching a loan over more years reduces monthly payments but multiplies the total interest paid.
  • Not accounting for variable rates. Some loans have rates that change over time. If your rate is variable, your monthly payment will change too. Calculate for the worst-case scenario.

How does interest rate affect monthly payments?

Higher interest rates mean higher monthly payments and more total interest paid. On a $20,000 five-year loan, the difference between 5% and 8% is about $30 more per month and over $1,800 more in total interest. Even small rate differences add up significantly over long loan terms like 15 or 30-year mortgages.

Should I choose a shorter or longer loan term?

Shorter terms have higher monthly payments but save significantly on total interest. Choose the shortest term you can comfortably afford. A good rule of thumb is to keep total debt payments under 36% of your gross monthly income while choosing the shortest term within that budget.

How do extra payments affect a loan?

Extra payments go directly toward reducing the principal. This lowers the amount of interest charged in subsequent months and can shorten your loan term by months or even years. For example, adding just $100 per month to a $200,000 mortgage at 6% over 30 years can save over $45,000 in interest and pay off the loan nearly 5 years early.

What is amortization?

Amortization is the process of spreading loan payments over time so that each payment covers both principal and interest. Early payments are mostly interest, while later payments are mostly principal. An amortization schedule shows you the exact breakdown for every payment over the life of the loan, which is useful for financial planning.

What is the difference between APR and interest rate?

The interest rate is the cost of borrowing the principal amount. The APR (Annual Percentage Rate) includes the interest rate plus additional fees like origination charges, closing costs, and mortgage insurance. APR gives you a more complete picture of the true cost of a loan, making it better for comparing offers from different lenders.

Conclusion

Calculating loan payments before you borrow is one of the smartest financial moves you can make. It helps you budget, compare offers, and avoid surprises. Whether you are financing a car, paying for school, or buying a home, knowing your numbers puts you in control.
