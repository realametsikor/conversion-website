How to Check Plagiarism for Free: A Complete Guide

Updated March 2026

Submitting work that contains unintentional plagiarism can lead to serious consequences in school and at work. Checking your writing before you hand it in is the simplest way to avoid problems. Free plagiarism checkers scan your text against billions of web pages and publications to flag any matching content. This guide explains how plagiarism detection works and how to use it effectively.

What Is a Plagiarism Checker?

A plagiarism checker is a tool that compares your text against a massive database of existing content, including websites, academic papers, books, and articles. It highlights any passages in your writing that match or closely resemble content found elsewhere. The tool then produces a report showing the percentage of original content and identifies the specific sources that overlap with your work.

These tools do not just look for exact word-for-word copies. Advanced checkers also detect paraphrased content, rearranged sentences, and synonym swaps that may still count as plagiarism in academic settings. Some tools also compare against previously submitted student papers, making it especially important for academic work.

Example

A student writes a paragraph about climate change and unknowingly uses a sentence nearly identical to one from a Wikipedia article they read during research. A plagiarism checker scans the paragraph, flags that sentence, and shows the original Wikipedia source. The student can then rewrite the sentence in their own words or add a proper citation before submitting.

Why Checking for Plagiarism Matters

  • Academic integrity: Schools and universities take plagiarism seriously. Getting caught can result in failing grades, academic probation, or even expulsion.
  • Professional credibility: Bloggers, journalists, and content writers risk their reputation if they publish duplicated material.
  • SEO performance: Search engines penalize websites with duplicate content. Original text ranks higher and attracts more traffic.
  • Legal protection: Copying someone else's published work can lead to copyright claims, especially in commercial contexts.
  • Self-improvement: Running your work through a checker helps you learn proper citation habits and develop your own writing voice.

Tip: Even experienced writers accidentally include phrases they have absorbed from their research. Running a quick plagiarism check is not a sign of distrust in your abilities -- it is a professional best practice that protects your credibility.

How to Check for Plagiarism: Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Open a free online plagiarism checker. Navigate to a browser-based tool that scans text for originality. No downloads or accounts needed.
  2. Paste or upload your text. Copy your essay, article, or report into the text box. Some tools also let you upload a Word document or PDF directly.
  3. Start the scan. Click "Check" or "Scan" to begin the analysis. The tool compares your text against its database, which usually takes 10 to 30 seconds.
  4. Review the results. The report highlights matching passages and shows the overall originality percentage. Aim for 90% or higher originality for academic work.
  5. Fix flagged sections. For any highlighted passages, either rewrite the content in your own words or add proper citations if you are quoting a source.
  6. Re-scan after editing. Run the checker again after making changes to confirm that your revisions resolved the flagged areas.

Example

A college student finishes a 2,000-word essay on renewable energy. They paste the text into a plagiarism checker and receive a report showing 88% originality. The report flags two sentences that closely match a published article. The student rewrites those sentences using their own phrasing, adds a citation for the original source, and re-scans. The second scan shows 97% originality, well within the acceptable range.

Tip: Aim for at least 90% originality on academic papers. Some universities require 85% or higher, while others have stricter thresholds. Check your institution's specific policy before submitting.

Tips and Best Practices

  • Check before every submission. Make plagiarism checking a regular part of your writing process, not just a one-time step. Build it into your workflow the same way you proofread for grammar.
  • Cite all sources properly. Even if you paraphrase, you must credit the original author. Use your required citation style (APA, MLA, Harvard, etc.). When in doubt, cite the source.
  • Write first, check later. Draft your content in your own words, then run the checker. This is more effective than trying to rewrite flagged content after the fact.
  • Use quotation marks for direct quotes. If you use someone's exact words, put them in quotation marks and include a citation. The checker will still flag them, but proper attribution shows they are intentional.
  • Paraphrase effectively. Do not just swap synonyms. Understand the original idea and express it in your own sentence structure and vocabulary. Read the source, close it, and then write from memory.
  • Keep a citation log while researching. As you read sources, record the author, title, publication, and page numbers immediately. This prevents lost citations when you write your paper later.
  • Use a text summarizer for research. When gathering information from multiple sources, use a summarization tool to help distill key points into your own notes before writing.

Example

Poor paraphrasing: Original says "Global temperatures have risen by 1.1 degrees Celsius since pre-industrial times." Student writes "Worldwide temperatures have increased by 1.1 degrees Celsius since pre-industrial times." This is too close to the original. Better paraphrasing: "Since the era before industrialization, the average temperature across the globe has climbed more than one degree Celsius, according to recent climate data." This version restructures the sentence and uses original phrasing.

Tip: The "read, close, write" technique is one of the most effective ways to paraphrase. Read the source material, close or hide it, and then write the idea entirely from your understanding. This naturally produces original phrasing.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Relying on one checker only. Different tools have different databases. For important submissions, consider scanning with more than one tool to catch anything the first one missed.
  • Ignoring small matches. Even a few matching phrases can add up. Address every flagged section to be safe, especially in academic work where even minor matches may raise concerns.
  • Confusing paraphrasing with plagiarism. Changing a few words in a sentence is not paraphrasing. You need to restructure the idea completely and still cite the source.
  • Skipping self-plagiarism checks. Reusing your own previously submitted work can also be flagged as plagiarism in academic settings. Check new work even if it covers similar topics to your previous papers.
  • Forgetting to check image sources. Plagiarism is not limited to text. If you include charts, diagrams, or images from other sources, you must credit them as well.

Tip: Self-plagiarism is a growing concern in academia. If you are writing about a topic you have covered before, start fresh with new research and new phrasing rather than copying from your own earlier submissions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What percentage of plagiarism is acceptable?

Most schools expect less than 10-15% similarity. Properly cited direct quotes may increase this number, which is usually acceptable as long as citations are correct. Aim for the highest originality score possible.

Can a plagiarism checker detect paraphrasing?

Advanced checkers can detect closely paraphrased content where only a few words have been changed. However, thoroughly rewritten content with original sentence structures typically passes cleanly.

Is my text stored when I use an online plagiarism checker?

This depends on the tool. Our plagiarism checker does not store your text. Always read the privacy policy of any tool before using it with sensitive academic or professional content.

Can I check plagiarism on my phone?

Yes. Online plagiarism checkers work on any device with a web browser. Paste your text, run the scan, and review results directly on your phone or tablet.

Does common knowledge count as plagiarism?

No. Facts that are widely known (like "water boils at 100 degrees Celsius") do not need citations. However, specific statistics, unique phrasing, or specialized analysis always need proper attribution.
