How to Compress a PDF Without Losing Quality

Updated March 2026

Large PDF files are hard to email, slow to upload, and take up valuable storage space. Whether you are a student submitting assignments, a professional sharing reports, or anyone who works with documents regularly, oversized PDFs can slow you down. You can compress a PDF to reduce its file size while keeping text, images, and formatting intact. This guide shows you exactly how to do it, what settings to use, and how to get the best results every time.

What Is PDF Compression?

PDF compression reduces the file size of a PDF document. It works by optimizing images, removing unused data, stripping out redundant metadata, and streamlining the internal file structure. A well-compressed PDF looks the same as the original but takes up significantly less space on your device or in the cloud.

There are two types of compression:

  • Lossless compression reduces file size without any drop in quality. All content stays identical. This method works by eliminating redundant data and optimizing how information is stored internally.
  • Lossy compression reduces size more aggressively by slightly lowering image resolution. Text stays sharp, but high-resolution photos may lose minor detail. This approach is best when you need the smallest possible file size and can tolerate slight visual differences in images.

Tip: For most everyday documents like reports, essays, and invoices, lossless compression is sufficient and preserves full quality. Only use lossy compression when your PDF contains many high-resolution photographs and you need dramatic size reduction.

Why You Might Need to Compress a PDF

  • Email attachment limits often cap at 10MB or 25MB. Compressed PDFs fit under these limits.
  • Uploading to web portals, job applications, or school submissions often requires smaller files.
  • Smaller PDFs load faster when shared as links or embedded on websites.
  • Saving storage space on your device or cloud drive.
  • Faster downloads for recipients, especially those on slower internet connections.

Example

You have a 15MB project report with embedded charts and photos that you need to email to your manager. Gmail allows up to 25MB, but your company email caps at 10MB. By compressing the PDF with medium settings, you reduce it to 4MB without any visible quality loss, making it easy to send as an attachment.

Step-by-Step Guide to Compressing a PDF

  1. Open a free online PDF compressor tool. No software installation needed.
  2. Click the upload button or drag and drop your PDF file into the tool.
  3. Select a compression level. Choose "medium" for a good balance of size and quality. If your PDF is mostly text, even low compression can yield great results.
  4. Click "Compress" and wait a few seconds for the tool to process your file.
  5. Download the compressed PDF. Compare the file size before and after to confirm the reduction.
  6. Open the compressed file and verify that text is readable, images look acceptable, and all pages are intact.

Tips and Best Practices

  • Start with medium compression. It gives the best balance. Only use high compression if you need the smallest possible file.
  • Check image quality after compression. Open the compressed file and zoom into images to verify they still look acceptable.
  • Remove unnecessary pages first. Use a PDF splitter to remove pages you do not need before compressing.
  • Avoid compressing already compressed PDFs. Running compression multiple times rarely helps and can reduce quality.
  • Use the right source format. If you are creating a PDF from a Word document, export it with optimized settings using our Word to PDF converter.
  • Resize images before adding them to your PDF. If you are creating the PDF yourself, scale down large photos to the dimensions you actually need before inserting them. A 4000x3000 pixel photo displayed at 800x600 wastes space.
  • Consider your audience. If the PDF will only be viewed on screen, 150 DPI images are sufficient. Print-quality PDFs need 300 DPI, but screen-only documents do not.
  • Use PDF/A format for archival documents. If long-term storage is your goal, compress the file and then save it in PDF/A format to ensure it remains accessible for years to come.

Tip: If you regularly work with large PDFs, establish a compression workflow. Compress files immediately after creating them so you never end up with an oversized backlog of documents taking up unnecessary storage space.

Example

A photographer exports a portfolio PDF with 50 high-resolution images at 35MB. By resizing the images to 1200 pixels wide before creating the PDF, and then applying medium compression, the final file comes in at just 5MB, perfect for emailing to potential clients without clogging their inbox.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much can I reduce a PDF file size?

It depends on the content. PDFs with large images can often be reduced by 50% to 80%. Text-heavy PDFs with few images may only shrink by 10% to 30%. Scanned documents, which are essentially full-page images, typically see the most dramatic reductions since image optimization has the greatest impact on those files.

Will compression affect text quality?

No. Text in PDFs is stored as vector data. Compression does not affect text clarity or readability. Only embedded images may be slightly affected at high compression levels. Even then, the difference is usually imperceptible at normal viewing sizes. If you stick with medium compression, both text and images will look virtually identical to the original.

Is it safe to compress PDFs online?

Yes, when you use a trusted tool. Our PDF compressor processes files in your browser. Nothing is uploaded to external servers. This means your sensitive documents, such as financial records or personal information, remain completely private throughout the compression process.

Can I compress a password-protected PDF?

Most online tools cannot compress encrypted PDFs. Remove the password first, compress the file, then re-add protection if needed. If you do not have the password, you will need to contact the document owner for an unprotected version.

What is the best file size for email attachments?

Keep PDF attachments under 5MB for most email providers. Gmail allows up to 25MB, but smaller files are faster to send and receive. Many corporate email systems have even stricter limits, sometimes as low as 10MB. When in doubt, aim for the smallest reasonable file size to ensure smooth delivery.
