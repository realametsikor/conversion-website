How to Convert Word to PDF on Any Device for Free

Updated March 2026

PDF is the go-to format when you need a document to look the same on every screen. Converting a Word file to PDF locks in your fonts, layout, and images so nothing shifts or breaks when someone else opens it. Whether you are on a laptop, tablet, or phone, this guide shows you how to convert Word to PDF quickly and for free.

What Is Word to PDF Conversion?

Word to PDF conversion takes a Microsoft Word document (.doc or .docx) and transforms it into a Portable Document Format (.pdf) file. The PDF version preserves your original formatting, including headings, tables, bullet points, images, and page margins. Unlike Word files, PDFs look identical regardless of which device, operating system, or software the reader uses.

This matters because Word documents can render differently depending on the version of Word or the fonts installed on the reader's computer. A PDF eliminates that uncertainty entirely. For instance, a document created in Microsoft Word 2024 on Windows may display differently in LibreOffice on Linux or in an older version of Word on macOS. Converting to PDF locks in the exact appearance you intended.

Example

Suppose you create a resume in Word using a two-column layout with custom fonts. When you email the .docx file, the hiring manager opens it on a Mac with different fonts installed. The columns shift, spacing breaks, and your carefully designed layout looks unprofessional. Converting to PDF before sending ensures the resume appears exactly as you designed it.

Why Convert Word Documents to PDF?

There are several practical reasons to save your Word files as PDFs:

  • Universal compatibility: Everyone can open a PDF, even without Microsoft Word installed. PDF readers are free and available on every platform.
  • Consistent appearance: Your document will look exactly the same on any device. No more misaligned text or missing fonts.
  • Professional submissions: Resumes, cover letters, proposals, and reports are best shared as PDFs to maintain a polished look.
  • Prevent accidental edits: PDFs are harder to modify than Word files, which protects your content from unintended changes.
  • Smaller file sizes: In many cases, the PDF version of a document is smaller than the original Word file, making it easier to email or upload.
  • Academic requirements: Many schools and universities require assignment submissions in PDF format.

Tip: If you are unsure which format to submit, PDF is almost always the safer choice. It works across all platforms and ensures your work looks professional.

Tips and Best Practices

  • Use common fonts. Stick to standard fonts like Arial, Times New Roman, or Calibri. Unusual fonts may not embed correctly in the PDF. If you must use a decorative or custom font, check whether the converter supports font embedding.
  • Check images before converting. Make sure all images in your Word document are high resolution. Low-quality images will look the same or worse in the PDF. For best results, use images that are at least 150 DPI for screen viewing and 300 DPI for print.
  • Set page margins correctly. Review your Word document's margins before converting. The PDF will mirror whatever margins you have set. Standard margins are typically 1 inch (2.54 cm) on all sides.
  • Remove tracked changes. Accept or reject all edits and comments in your Word document before conversion. Otherwise, markup and revision notes may appear in the PDF.
  • Update your table of contents. If your document has a table of contents, refresh it in Word before converting so page numbers match correctly.
  • Compress if needed. If the resulting PDF is too large, use a PDF compressor to reduce the file size without noticeable quality loss.
  • Test on multiple devices. After converting, open the PDF on your phone and a different computer to verify it displays correctly everywhere.

Tip: Before converting a long document, save a backup of your original Word file. This way, if you need to make changes later, you still have the fully editable source file.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not reviewing the PDF after conversion. Always open and check the converted file. Occasionally, complex layouts or embedded objects may shift. Pay special attention to headers, footers, and page breaks.
  • Using custom fonts without embedding. If your Word document uses fonts that are not standard, they may not display correctly in the PDF. Embed fonts or switch to common alternatives before converting.
  • Leaving hyperlinks unchecked. Clickable links in Word usually carry over to PDF, but verify that they still work after conversion. Broken links in a professional document leave a poor impression.
  • Ignoring file size. Documents with many images can produce large PDFs. Compress images in Word before converting, or use a compressor tool afterward.
  • Converting drafts instead of final versions. Make sure you are converting the final, proofread version of your document. It is easy to accidentally convert an earlier draft with errors still present.

Example

The professor sees the markup and asks the student to resubmit. Always accept or reject all changes and delete comments before converting to PDF.

What is the difference between .doc and .docx?
