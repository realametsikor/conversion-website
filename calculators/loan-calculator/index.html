💰 What Is a Loan Calculator?

A loan calculator uses the standard amortisation formula to estimate how much you will pay each month on a fixed-rate loan, how much of that goes to interest, and what the loan will cost you in total over its lifetime.

Understanding these numbers before taking out a loan is one of the most important financial decisions you can make. A $200,000 loan at 5% interest over 30 years costs $186,511 in interest alone — nearly as much again as the original loan. Knowing this upfront lets you compare lenders, negotiate better terms, and decide whether to take a shorter loan term.

Standard Amortisation Formula
M = P × [r(1+r)^n] ÷ [(1+r)^n - 1]
M = Monthly payment  |  P = Principal loan amount
r = Monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12)
n = Total number of monthly payments (years × 12)

Example: $200,000 loan at 6% for 30 years → r = 0.005, n = 360 → M = $1,199/month

✅ Tips for Managing Your Loan Effectively

  • 1
    Shop for the lowest interest rate before committing. Even 0.5% difference in rate can save thousands over the life of a loan. Get quotes from at least 3 lenders and compare the APR, not just the interest rate.
  • 2
    Make extra payments when possible. Any amount paid above your minimum monthly payment goes directly to reducing your principal — dramatically cutting total interest and shortening your loan term.
  • 3
    Understand APR vs interest rate. The Annual Percentage Rate (APR) includes fees and other costs, making it the true cost of borrowing. Always compare APR — not just the headline interest rate — when evaluating loan offers.
  • 4
    Consider a larger down payment. For mortgages and auto loans, a larger down payment reduces your principal, which directly lowers both your monthly payment and total interest paid.
  • 5
    Improve your credit score before applying. Borrowers with higher credit scores qualify for significantly lower interest rates. Even a 50-point improvement can save thousands over the life of a large loan.
  • 6
    Use this calculator to stress-test your budget. Calculate what your monthly payment would be if rates rise by 1–2%. If the higher payment would strain your budget, consider a smaller loan or longer term as a buffer.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What formula does this loan calculator use?
This calculator uses the standard amortisation formula: M = P × [r(1+r)^n] ÷ [(1+r)^n - 1], where M is the monthly payment, P is the principal loan amount, r is the monthly interest rate (annual rate divided by 12), and n is the total number of monthly payments (years multiplied by 12). This is the same formula used by banks and financial institutions worldwide.
Does this include taxes and insurance?
No. This calculator computes principal and interest only. For mortgage loans, your actual monthly payment will be higher when property taxes, homeowner's insurance, and private mortgage insurance (PMI) are included. Contact your lender for a full payment breakdown including these costs.
Can I use this for different types of loans?
Yes. This calculator works for any fixed-rate amortising loan — including mortgages, auto loans, personal loans, student loans, and business loans. It assumes equal monthly payments and a fixed interest rate for the entire loan term. It does not account for variable-rate loans where the rate changes over time.
What is amortisation?
Amortisation is the process of paying off a loan through regular equal monthly payments over time. Each payment covers both the interest owed for that month and a portion of the principal balance. In the early months, most of your payment goes to interest. As the principal decreases, more of each payment goes to the principal — until the loan is paid off.
How does interest rate affect my monthly payment?
Even small differences in interest rate have a large impact on total cost. On a $200,000 30-year mortgage, the difference between 5% and 7% interest adds about $257 to the monthly payment — and over $92,000 in total interest paid over the life of the loan. Always shop for the lowest rate possible.
Should I choose a 15-year or 30-year mortgage?
A 15-year mortgage has higher monthly payments but saves significantly on total interest — often $100,000 or more on a standard loan. A 30-year mortgage has lower monthly payments, giving you more monthly cash flow flexibility. Use this calculator to compare both options with your specific loan amount and rate to make the right decision for your budget.
Is my financial data private?
Yes — completely. All calculations happen entirely in your browser using JavaScript. Your loan amounts, income details, and financial figures are never sent to any server or stored anywhere. When you close the tab, the data is gone.
What if my interest rate is 0%?
If the interest rate is 0%, the calculator divides the loan amount by the total number of months to calculate the monthly payment, with zero total interest charged. This applies to some promotional financing offers on vehicles and electronics.
