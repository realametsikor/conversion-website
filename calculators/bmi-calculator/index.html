🧮 What Is BMI?

BMI — Body Mass Index — is a numerical value calculated from your height and weight. It was developed in the 1830s by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet as a simple population-level screening tool. Today it is used by doctors and health organisations worldwide as an initial indicator of whether a person's weight may be putting their health at risk.

BMI does not directly measure body fat. Instead it uses weight and height as proxies, making it a fast and cost-free screening method that requires no equipment. A full health assessment from a doctor will give you a more complete picture.

BMI Formula
Metric: BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height² (m)
Imperial: BMI = (weight (lbs) ÷ height² (in)) × 703
Example: A person weighing 70kg at 1.75m tall has a BMI of 70 ÷ (1.75 × 1.75) = 22.9

⚠️ BMI Limitations — What It Doesn't Tell You

BMI is a useful starting point but it has well-documented limitations. Understanding these helps you interpret your result correctly.

Group Why BMI May Be Misleading Better Measure
Athletes High muscle mass increases weight without increasing fat, pushing BMI into "overweight" despite being very fit Body fat percentage
Elderly people Muscle loss with age means BMI can read "normal" while body fat percentage is actually high Waist circumference + fat %
Pregnant women Weight gain during pregnancy is normal and expected — BMI during pregnancy is not a useful indicator Gestational weight charts
Children Standard adult BMI categories do not apply to children — growth stage and sex must be considered BMI-for-age percentile charts
Different ethnicities Health risks associated with BMI vary by ethnicity — some populations face higher risks at lower BMI values Ethnicity-adjusted guidelines
Important: BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnosis. If your result falls outside the normal range, speak to a qualified healthcare professional before making changes to your diet or exercise habits.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What is BMI and how is it calculated?
BMI (Body Mass Index) is calculated by dividing your weight in kilograms by the square of your height in metres (kg/m²). In imperial units, the formula is (weight in lbs ÷ height in inches²) × 703. Our calculator handles this automatically — just enter your weight and height.
What is a healthy BMI for adults?
For most adults, a BMI between 18.5 and 24.9 is considered healthy by the World Health Organisation. However, optimal ranges can vary by age, sex, and ethnicity. Always consult a healthcare provider for personalised guidance.
Is BMI accurate for everyone?
BMI is a useful population-level screening tool but has limitations. It does not distinguish between muscle and fat mass, so athletes and very muscular people often score in the "overweight" range despite being in excellent health. Similarly, elderly individuals may score "normal" while having a high body fat percentage. It is best used as one data point alongside other health measures.
What BMI range is considered obese?
A BMI of 30.0 or above is classified as obese. This is further divided into three classes: Class I (30–34.9), Class II (35–39.9), and Class III or severe obesity (40 and above). Each class carries progressively higher health risks. If your BMI falls in this range, speaking to a doctor is recommended.
Can I use this BMI calculator for children?
Standard adult BMI categories do not apply to children and teenagers. Children's BMI must be interpreted using age and sex-specific growth charts (BMI-for-age percentile). This calculator is designed for adults aged 18 and over.
Is my data private?
Yes — completely. All calculations happen entirely in your browser using JavaScript. Your weight, height, age, and BMI result are never stored, shared, or sent to any server. When you close the tab, the data is gone.
What is the difference between metric and imperial BMI?
The calculation is the same — only the input units differ. Metric uses kilograms and centimetres; imperial uses pounds and feet/inches. Our calculator converts automatically so the result is identical regardless of which system you choose.
How often should I check my BMI?
There is no strict rule, but checking monthly is reasonable if you are actively managing your weight. For general wellness tracking, quarterly checks are sufficient. BMI alone is not a day-to-day health metric — focus on trends over time rather than individual readings.
